Reversed Cards: Should You Use Them?

By Michael Wylde · March 7, 2026 · 7 min read

No question in tarot sparks more passionate disagreement than this one. You pull a card from the spread and it appears upside-down -- rotated 180 degrees from its usual orientation. Some readers treat this as a critical piece of information. Others flip it right-side up without a second thought. Both camps have been practicing successfully for centuries, which makes the debate all the more interesting.

If you are new to tarot, the reversal question can feel paralyzing. If you are experienced, you probably have strong feelings about it already. Either way, it is worth understanding what reversed cards actually mean, why people disagree, and how to make the choice that serves your practice best.

What Happens When a Card Is Reversed

A reversal simply means the card appears upside-down in the spread. In a physical reading, this happens naturally during shuffling -- some cards rotate as they slide past each other, and by the time you lay them out, a portion of the deck has flipped. In a digital reading, reversals are either enabled or disabled by the software, and the orientation is determined by whatever randomization engine drives the shuffle.

The card itself does not change. The Empress reversed is still the Empress. The imagery is the same, the numerology is the same, the elemental associations are the same. What changes is how the reader interprets that card's energy in the context of the question and position. And this is where the three major schools of thought diverge.

Three Ways to Read a Reversal

1. Opposite or Blocked Energy

The most common interpretation, and the one most beginners learn first, treats a reversal as the negation or blockage of the card's upright meaning. The Empress upright represents abundance, nurturing, creative fertility. The Empress reversed, in this framework, suggests stagnation, creative blocks, neglect of self-care, or withholding of nurture.

This approach is intuitive and gives clear, decisive meanings. It is also the most likely to produce stark, uncomfortable readings -- which some readers consider a feature, not a bug.

2. Internalized or Private Expression

A more nuanced school of thought reads reversals as the card's energy turned inward. The upright card expresses itself outwardly and visibly. The reversed card expresses the same energy, but privately, internally, or in ways that are not yet visible to others.

The Empress reversed, in this reading, does not mean the absence of nurturing. It means nurturing directed toward the self rather than others, or creative energy that is gestating quietly rather than manifesting publicly. This approach tends to produce gentler, more psychologically textured readings.

3. Delayed or Weakened Expression

The third school treats reversals as a matter of degree and timing. The energy of the card is present, but it is arriving slowly, expressing itself at reduced intensity, or encountering friction. The Empress reversed is still the Empress -- her gifts are still available -- but they may take longer to materialize, or they may arrive in a diminished form.

This interpretation works particularly well in predictive spreads where timing matters, and it avoids the binary quality that the "opposite meaning" approach can sometimes impose.

A reversal is not a punishment. It is a modifier -- a way of saying "this energy is present, but it is not expressing itself straightforwardly."

The Case for Using Reversals

Readers who use reversals will tell you the benefits are substantial. The most obvious: reversals double the interpretive range of the deck. A 78-card deck becomes, effectively, a system with 156 possible states. Each card gains a second voice, a shadow register, a way of speaking about difficulty and complexity that the upright meaning alone may not capture.

Reversals also add nuance to difficult cards. The Ten of Swords upright is one of the most alarming images in the deck -- a figure pinned to the ground by ten blades. But the Ten of Swords reversed often signals that the worst is over, that recovery has begun, that the crisis is lifting. Without reversals, you lose that distinction.

There is a traditional argument as well. Many historical tarot lineages, including the Rider-Waite-Smith tradition that most modern readers learned from, explicitly included reversed meanings. Arthur Edward Waite devoted significant space to reversals in The Pictorial Key to the Tarot, and the tradition of reading inversions stretches back to the cartomancy practices that preceded tarot.

Perhaps most importantly, reversals prevent uniformly positive readings. If you are reading upright-only and you draw the Sun, the Star, and the Ten of Cups, the reading feels wonderful -- but is it honest? Life contains friction, delay, and shadow. Reversals ensure the cards can speak to the full spectrum of experience, not just its highlights.

The Case Against Reversals

The opposing camp is equally well-reasoned. Their central argument is elegant: every upright card already contains its shadow. The Moon upright speaks of illusion, confusion, and fear alongside intuition and dreams. The Seven of Cups upright is already about fantasy, temptation, and scattered energy. You do not need to flip these cards upside-down to access their difficult dimensions -- those dimensions are built into the image.

Critics also argue that reversals add complexity without adding clarity. When every card can mean two different things depending on orientation, the interpretive space becomes so wide that the reader can justify almost any narrative. Paradoxically, having more options can make readings less specific, not more.

Several major tarot traditions do not use reversals at all. Readers of the Marseille tarot, one of the oldest European traditions, traditionally read all cards upright. Aleister Crowley's Thoth tarot was designed without reversed meanings. These are not simplified or beginner systems -- they are sophisticated, centuries-old frameworks that consider reversals unnecessary.

There is also the emotional dimension. Reversals can feel punitive, especially to people who are new to tarot or coming to a reading during a difficult period. Drawing three reversed cards in a row can create anxiety that overshadows the reading's actual message. Some readers find that removing reversals creates a safer, more open space for honest interpretation.

The absence of reversals is not the absence of shadow. Every card in the deck already holds both light and darkness. The question is whether you need a visual cue to access the darker reading.

The Middle Path

Here is what experienced readers often arrive at after years of practice: reversals are a tool, not a rule. You can use them when they serve the reading and set them aside when they do not.

Many readers enable reversals when they want more specificity -- when the question is precise and they want the cards to offer granular, directional guidance. A career reading about a specific job offer, a relationship reading about a particular conflict, a health reading about a defined symptom -- these benefit from the additional nuance that reversals provide.

Those same readers might disable reversals when they want broader archetypal guidance -- when the question is open-ended and they are seeking themes, patterns, and big-picture wisdom rather than pointed advice. A reading about life purpose, a seasonal reflection, a meditation on a creative project -- these can breathe more freely without the additional layer of reversal interpretation.

The key insight is that neither approach is more "advanced" or more "authentic." They are different lenses, suited to different questions. The best readers are fluent in both.

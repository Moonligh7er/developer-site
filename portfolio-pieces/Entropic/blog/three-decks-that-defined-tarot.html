The Three Decks That Defined Tarot: 1491, 1760, 1909

By Michael Wylde · March 14, 2026 · 9 min read

Five centuries of tarot history can be distilled into three decisive moments -- three decks that each redefined what the cards were, what they looked like, and what they were for. Every tarot deck you have ever held, every app you have ever used, every interpretation you have ever read descends from one or more of these three ancestors. Understanding them is not academic trivia. It is understanding the DNA of the tradition itself.

The Sola Busca: 1491

The First Complete Tarot

In the final decade of the fifteenth century, somewhere in northern Italy, an unknown artist created a set of seventy-eight engraved copper plates depicting warriors, alchemists, and classical heroes. The deck was commissioned by or for the Sola Busca family of Venetian nobility. It is the oldest known complete tarot deck -- all seventy-eight cards, Major and Minor Arcana, surviving intact to this day. The originals are held in the Pinacoteca di Brera in Milan.

What makes the Sola Busca revolutionary is not merely its age. It is the fact that every single card -- including the pip cards of the Minor Arcana -- is illustrated with a unique narrative scene. This was extraordinary. For centuries afterward, most tarot decks would depict the Minor Arcana as simple arrangements of suit symbols: four cups in a row, seven swords arranged geometrically, ten pentacles in a pattern. The Sola Busca rejected this convention before it even fully existed.

The Sola Busca deck illustrated every Minor Arcana card with a human scene four hundred years before the idea became standard. It was ahead of its own future.

Alchemy and the Classical World

The deck's imagery draws heavily from two sources: Renaissance alchemy and Roman history. The trump cards (what we now call the Major Arcana) do not follow the familiar sequence of Fool, Magician, High Priestess, and so on. Instead, they depict historical and mythological figures -- Nero, Carbone, Lentulo, Metelo -- each associated with alchemical processes and classical virtues. The suits are populated with warriors and scholars engaged in scenes of conflict, contemplation, and transformation.

The alchemical dimension is significant. This was a deck created in the same intellectual milieu that produced the Corpus Hermeticum, Marsilio Ficino's translations of Plato, and Pico della Mirandola's synthesis of Christian, Jewish, and pagan mysticism. The Sola Busca is a tarot deck born from the same crucible that would eventually produce the Western esoteric tradition as we know it.

A Hidden Influence

The Sola Busca might have remained a historical curiosity were it not for one crucial detail: in 1907, the British Museum acquired a complete set of photographic reproductions. These photographs were available for public viewing. Two years later, a young artist named Pamela Colman Smith was working in London on a new tarot deck. The influence of the Sola Busca on her work is unmistakable. Her Three of Swords -- a heart pierced by three blades against a stormy sky -- closely mirrors the Sola Busca's own Three of Swords. Her Ten of Wands, depicting a figure burdened by a heavy load, echoes the same card in the Italian original. The oldest complete tarot deck reached forward across four centuries to shape the most popular tarot deck ever created.

The Tarot de Marseille: 1760

Nicolas Conver's Definitive Edition

By the eighteenth century, tarot had migrated from Italian aristocratic courts to French card-makers' workshops. Dozens of regional variants existed, but in 1760, a master card-maker named Nicolas Conver produced a deck in Marseille that would become the definitive standard for what we now call the Tarot de Marseille tradition. Conver did not invent this style -- earlier versions by Jean Dodal (c. 1701) and Jean-Pierre Payen (c. 1713) established the basic template. But Conver refined it to such a degree of clarity and consistency that his version became the benchmark against which all others were measured.

The Tarot de Marseille is visually unmistakable. Bold primary colors -- red, blue, yellow, and flesh tones -- are applied in flat, unshaded blocks. The lines are heavy and confident, cut from woodblocks with the directness of a folk art tradition that valued legibility over subtlety. The Major Arcana figures are frontal, iconic, almost heraldic. Le Bateleur (The Magician) stands at his table with his tools laid out before him. La Papesse (The High Priestess) sits between two columns, an open book on her lap. Le Pendu (The Hanged Man) dangles serenely from his gallows.

The Tarot de Marseille does not illustrate. It presents. Its images are not scenes to be read like stories. They are symbols to be contemplated like icons.

The Unillustrated Pips

The Minor Arcana of the Tarot de Marseille are strictly geometric. The Five of Cups is five cups arranged in a pattern. The Eight of Swords is eight swords interlocked in an elaborate symmetrical design. The Ten of Coins is ten coins arranged in the shape of the Qabalistic Tree of Life -- a detail that suggests deeper esoteric knowledge embedded in what appears to be simple decorative arrangement. There are no human figures, no narrative scenes, no emotional cues. The pip cards are pure pattern.

This is not a limitation. It is a philosophy. The Tarot de Marseille tradition holds that meaning emerges from the interaction of number, element, and position -- not from pictorial storytelling. A reader trained in this tradition does not need a picture of a sad figure to know that the Five of Cups represents loss. They know it because five is the number of disruption in the system of the Sephiroth, and Cups is the suit of emotion. The meaning is structural, not illustrative.

The Cartomantic Tradition

It was in the orbit of the Tarot de Marseille that tarot first became widely associated with divination. In 1781, Antoine Court de Gebelin published his famous (and largely fanciful) essay claiming that tarot originated in ancient Egypt and encoded the secret wisdom of Thoth. In 1783, the professional fortune-teller Jean-Baptiste Alliette -- working under the pseudonym Etteilla -- published the first known guide to reading tarot cards for divinatory purposes. Both men worked from the Marseille tradition. The deck that defined how tarot looked also defined what tarot was for.

The Marseille tradition remains alive today, particularly in France, Spain, and Latin America. Many contemporary readers consider it the most "authentic" form of tarot, uncorrupted by the Victorian occultist overlay that would come later. Whether or not that claim holds up to scrutiny, there is no denying the Tarot de Marseille's foundational status. It is the trunk of the tree.

The Smith-Waite: 1909

Pamela Colman Smith's Revolution

In 1909, the London publishing house Rider and Company released a tarot deck that would become the most widely used in the English-speaking world and, eventually, on the planet. It was commissioned by Arthur Edward Waite, a scholar and initiate of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, and illustrated by Pamela Colman Smith, a mixed-race Anglo-Jamaican artist, storyteller, and fellow Golden Dawn member. For decades it was called the "Rider-Waite" deck. In recent years, scholars and practitioners have increasingly adopted the name Smith-Waite to properly credit the artist whose work made it iconic.

Smith's contribution was not incremental. It was revolutionary. She did what the Sola Busca had done four centuries earlier, but with a coherence and emotional intelligence that the Renaissance deck never achieved: she illustrated every single Minor Arcana card with a narrative scene.

Before Smith, the Six of Cups was six cups. After Smith, it was a child handing a flower to another child in a sunlit garden. She gave the Minor Arcana a human face, and tarot was never the same.

The Golden Dawn System Made Visible

Smith was not working from pure artistic inspiration. Waite provided her with detailed instructions based on the Golden Dawn's system of correspondences -- the network of associations linking each card to an element, a planet or zodiac sign, a Hebrew letter, a position on the Tree of Life, and a specific interpretive meaning. Smith's genius was translating this abstract symbolic architecture into images that felt immediate, emotional, and human.

The Three of Swords -- associated with Saturn in Libra, the third Sephirah of Binah in the suit of Air, signifying sorrow -- became a pierced heart under rain. The Ten of Pentacles -- associated with Mercury in Virgo, the tenth Sephirah of Malkuth in the suit of Earth, signifying material completion -- became a multi-generational family scene beneath an archway inscribed with the Tree of Life. The Four of Cups -- Jupiter in Cancer, Chesed in Water, signifying emotional stagnation -- became a young man sitting under a tree, ignoring a cup offered by a disembodied hand.

Each image is a compression of an entire network of esoteric meaning into a single scene that can be read intuitively by someone who has never heard of the Golden Dawn. This was Smith's revolution: she made the esoteric exoteric without dumbing it down.

The Democratization of Tarot

The practical consequence of illustrating every card was enormous. Before Smith-Waite, reading the Minor Arcana required years of study -- memorizing numerical and elemental correspondences, internalizing the Qabalistic system, learning to derive meaning from abstract combinations. After Smith-Waite, a beginner could look at the Five of Pentacles -- two ragged figures trudging through snow past a lit church window -- and immediately grasp its meaning: hardship, exclusion, material suffering, and the possibility of help that goes unnoticed.

This democratization transformed tarot from an esoteric discipline practiced by initiates into a popular practice accessible to anyone with a deck and a guidebook. The vast majority of tarot decks published in the last century are based on the Smith-Waite template. Its imagery has become the visual language of tarot itself. When someone says "tarot card," the image that appears in most minds is a Smith-Waite image.

Three Philosophies of the Card

These three decks do not merely represent three moments in history. They represent three fundamentally different answers to the question: what is a tarot card?

The Sola Busca says a card is a vessel for encoded knowledge -- alchemical, classical, esoteric. Its images are puzzles addressed to the learned. The Tarot de Marseille says a card is a symbolic structure -- number, element, and archetype, expressed through pattern rather than narrative. Its images are contemplative objects. The Smith-Waite says a card is a story -- a human scene that speaks directly to experience and emotion. Its images are mirrors.

None of these approaches is wrong. Each illuminates something the others leave in shadow. The Sola Busca reminds us that tarot has always been entangled with the deepest currents of Western intellectual history. The Tarot de Marseille reminds us that the structure beneath the images -- the mathematics of number, element, and position -- carries meaning independent of any picture. The Smith-Waite reminds us that all the esoteric architecture in the world means nothing if it cannot speak to a human being sitting alone with a question and a deck of cards.
